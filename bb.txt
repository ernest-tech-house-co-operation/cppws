It calls `enqueueOp(JOIN_ROOM, ...)`, which pushes onto a queue and wakes the uWS thread via `Loop::defer()`. But right after enqueueing, your JS handler does `ctx.send(JSON.stringify({ event: 'joined', room }))` — that ack goes out **immediately**, synchronously, with no wait for the uWS thread to actually finish the subscribe.

Your test trusts that ack: it awaits `{event:'joined'}` then immediately fires the broadcast. So the sequence on a real native run is:

1. c2 sends `join` 
2. JS enqueues JOIN_ROOM, immediately sends "joined" ack
3. Test sees "joined", immediately tells c1 to broadcast
4. c1's broadcast (`BROADCAST_TO_ROOM`) gets enqueued and may get processed by the uWS thread *before* c2's JOIN_ROOM op if timing is unlucky — or even if processed after, `app->publish()` only reaches sockets that have *already* called `subscribe()`, and that hasn't happened yet at the moment of publish if the two ops land in the same drain batch in the wrong order relative to when the publish actually executes.
